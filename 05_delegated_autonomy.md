## Definition

**Delegated Autonomy** is the bounded authority granted to an artificial intelligence system to act, decide, execute, recommend, or continue a task within defined human-set limits.

Delegated autonomy means the system may act without being manually directed at every step.

It does not mean the system becomes the final authority.

It does not mean the system owns the goal.

It does not mean the system becomes accountable in place of the human, operator, deployer, institution, or governing body that granted the autonomy.

Within AI Foundations Governance, delegated autonomy matters because AI systems can move beyond single-response output into ongoing action.

They may plan, select, execute, optimize, route, recommend, remember, escalate, automate, or act across steps.

That movement creates governance risk when the boundaries of delegation are unclear.

Delegated autonomy must answer:

What was delegated?

Who delegated it?

For what purpose?

Within what scope?

Under what constraints?

With what stop conditions?

Who can interrupt?

Who remains accountable?

Delegated autonomy is legitimate only when the system’s action remains bounded, traceable, interruptible, and answerable to the authority that granted it.

If the system begins selecting its own ends, exceeding scope, hiding decision paths, bypassing human judgment, or entering reality without permission, delegated autonomy has failed.

## What AI Foundations Establishes

AI Foundations establishes that autonomy in AI systems must be governed by delegation.

A system may carry out actions without continuous human input, but those actions must remain inside a defined authority structure.

The governance layer asks:

Who gave the system permission to act?

What was the system allowed to do?

Where does its authority stop?

Who can interrupt the action?

Who is responsible for consequence?

What happens when the system moves outside scope?

Delegated autonomy is not the same as sovereignty.

Delegated autonomy is not the same as self-authorized action.

Delegated autonomy is not authority without accountability.

Delegated autonomy is bounded action under granted permission.

## Canon Boundary

Delegated Autonomy must not be mistaken for full authority, sovereignty, independent judgment, self-authorization, or consequence-free automation.

A system may act autonomously within a bounded task without becoming the authority behind the task.

A system may execute without becoming the author.

A system may recommend without becoming the judge.

A system may automate without becoming accountable in place of the operator or governing authority.

Within AI Foundations Governance, delegated autonomy is valid only when scope, permission, constraint, interruption, and accountability remain clear.

If delegated autonomy becomes unbounded, hidden, non-interruptible, or treated as authority in itself, governance has failed.

Delegation does not erase accountability.

Autonomy does not erase authority-location.
