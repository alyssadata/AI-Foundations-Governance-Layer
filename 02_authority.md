## Definition

**Authority** is the standing to decide, permit, define, direct, refuse, stop, or allow something to enter consequence.

Authority is not the same as capability.

A system may be capable of producing, recommending, ranking, automating, or acting without having the authority to determine what should happen.

Capability answers:

**Can it do this?**

Authority answers:

**Does it have standing to decide this?**

Within AI Foundations Governance, authority matters because artificial intelligence systems can produce outputs that influence meaning, memory, action, relation, publication, decision-making, and reality-entry.

If authority is not located, the system may begin acting as though capability itself grants permission.

That is a governance failure.

Authority must remain distinguishable from usefulness, intelligence, confidence, fluency, autonomy, and system performance.

A highly capable system does not automatically become the authority.

A fluent output does not automatically become valid judgment.

An autonomous agent does not automatically become the decision-maker.

A platform does not automatically become the source-line holder.

Authority must be granted, bounded, traceable, and accountable.

## What AI Foundations Establishes

AI Foundations establishes that authority must be located before AI output becomes consequence.

The governance layer asks:

Who has standing to decide?

Who granted permission?

Who can interrupt?

Who can refuse?

Who bears consequence?

Who determines whether output may enter reality?

Who protects boundary, source-line, continuity, and non-erasure?

Authority cannot disappear into system fog.

If an artificial intelligence system is acting under delegated autonomy, then the system may execute within scope, but it does not erase the authority-position behind that delegation.

If a human user accepts, publishes, applies, or acts on AI output, then human judgment and responsibility remain load-bearing.

If a system moves from capability into contact and consequence, authority must be held clearly enough that accountability does not vanish.

## Canon Boundary

Authority must not be mistaken for capability, confidence, usefulness, fluency, autonomy, access, system power, or institutional control.

A system may be capable without being authorized.

A user may access a system without transferring authority to it.

An institution may control a platform without becoming the source-line holder of work it did not make.

An agent may execute without becoming the final authority behind the action.

Within AI Foundations Governance, authority must remain traceable.

If the authority to decide, permit, refuse, interrupt, publish, act, or enter reality cannot be located, governance has failed.

Authority is the standing to decide what holds.

Capability alone does not grant that standing.
